NeverRanked · Teardown 02 · Cross-category

Why your category’s AI-citation pattern depends on whether you’re a commodity or a relationship business.

4 Hawaii business categories, 4 cohorts, same 7-AI-tool methodology, same 18-question discipline. The own-site citation share moves from 53% in banking to 27% in wealth management. The reason is structural, and the implication for where to do the work is the point.

The headline finding in one sentence: the more your category looks like a commodity (a checking account, the kind of thing buyers compare on rates and convenience), the more AI tools cite your own website when someone asks for a recommendation. The more your category looks like a relationship business (a wealth manager, the kind of thing buyers pick on trust and personality), the more AI cites third-party content about you instead.

What this means in plain English

Forget the percentages for a second. When a buyer asks ChatGPT or Google AI for a Hawaii bank recommendation, the AI answers mostly by quoting from the banks’ own websites. When that same buyer asks for a Hawaii wealth manager, the AI answers mostly by quoting from articles, lists, and publications that talk about the wealth managers. Same kind of buyer, same kind of question, very different sources.

The reason looks structural. Banking is a commodity. The features (rates, account types, branch locations) live cleanly on the bank’s own site, in a format AI can parse, refreshed regularly. There is no need to look anywhere else. Wealth management is the opposite. The features that matter (the founder’s judgment, the firm’s philosophy, their track record with people like you) do not live cleanly on the firm’s own homepage. They live in interviews, in published rankings, in client stories, in podcasts. So that is where AI looks.

Dental sits between banking and the wealth/law tier. Dental leans toward the commodity end (clinical procedures, insurance acceptance, hours and location are commodity-shaped facts the practice can publish cleanly on its own site). Wealth management and law firms cluster together at the relationship end: a buyer choosing a wealth manager or law firm wants to read about the named principal, their reputation, their wins, which lives in editorial sources more than on the firm’s homepage. Both wealth and law have meaningful share on both surfaces, neither dominates.

The implication for where to do the work

If you are a Hawaii consumer bank, AEO work that focuses on your own website pays off. Clean structured data, clear product pages, good FAQ markup. AI is already reading you. Make the read cleaner.

If you are a Hawaii wealth manager, that same work largely will not move the needle. AI is barely citing your website to begin with. The leverage is off-site: get into the lists AI reads, get into the publications AI cites, get into the named-author content AI surfaces. The own-site work is hygiene, not the lever.

Dental and law: both surfaces matter. Own-site optimization is necessary but not sufficient. The list of fixes for these categories is twice as long because two distinct workstreams are open at the same time.

The non-obvious read: the conventional SEO instinct (“clean up your website first, everything else is secondary”) is approximately correct for commodity categories and approximately wrong for relationship categories. Which is which depends on the structural shape of the category, not on industry conventional wisdom.

The Microsoft Copilot pattern

One pattern is consistent across all four categories. Microsoft Copilot (which answers using Bing’s search results) cites the firms’ own websites less than 1% of the time in every category we measured.

This means: a buyer who asks Microsoft Copilot for a Hawaii business recommendation in any of these four categories gets pointed at third-party content (publications, Wikipedia, directories), almost never at the businesses’ own websites. Every business in every category we measured shares this gap.

The first-mover read: whichever firm shows up first in Bing’s organic search results for these queries gets the Copilot answer mostly to itself, because every competitor is also invisible there. The gap is open, and it does not appear to be a category-specific problem. It is a Microsoft Copilot pattern across local-service queries.

Where AI knows you by name (Claude and Gemma)

Two of the seven AI tools we measure (Claude and Gemma) answer from training data instead of searching the live web. So when they mention a business by name, it means that business has formed a presence in the AI’s training data: through Wikipedia, news coverage, named-founder content, sustained editorial presence over years.

This is the slowest signal in the measurement, but the most defensible once it forms. A new competitor cannot show up in Claude’s training data overnight. That presence is earned through years of editorial work that an AI model later learned from.

The same gradient holds inside Claude and Gemma. Banking firms have the strongest training-data presence. Wealth firms have the weakest. Dental and law cluster in the middle.

The asymmetry within rows is worth noting too. Claude and Gemma do not always agree on who is in their training data. Claude may know a firm Gemma does not, and vice versa. The measurement captures both because the buyer might use either.

The cohort sizes (transparency note)

The four cohorts were not the same size. Cohort size is determined by how many distinct businesses AI tools mention with enough frequency to register as recurring. Categories with more independent businesses (dental practices, individual law firms) surface larger cohorts. Categories with consolidation (banking, wealth management) surface smaller ones. This is a feature of the measurement, not a bias: the cohort reflects who AI actually cites, not who we picked in advance.

  • Hawaii consumer banking: 21 banks and credit unions, named in full at teardown 01.

Methodology summary

Each category was measured the same way:

  • 7 AI tools: Perplexity, ChatGPT search (gpt-4o-mini-search-preview), Gemini grounded (gemini-2.5-flash with Google search), Microsoft Copilot via Bing organic search, Google AI Overviews, Claude (claude-haiku-4-5), Gemma (open-weight, via Together AI).
  • 18 questions per category, locked by hash so every run compares apples to apples. The questions mirror how a real buyer in each category would actually search.
  • 3 repetitions per question per AI tool to separate signal from noise.
  • 2 to 3 measurement runs per category on this dataset. Banking and dental have 3 usable runs each (pattern-readiness cleared per MOAT.md rule 5). Law and wealth management have 2 usable runs each. The cross-category gradient is consistent across all four categories. Dental's numbers held identical from run 2 to run 3 (stability signal). Wealth's number rose from 27% to 37% between runs as 8 additional firms surfaced in cohort coverage, which is the cohort-coverage discipline working as designed: more runs surface more cohort members, the share trends toward true ceiling.

Full methodology, source bucket definitions, and pattern-readiness rules at /methodology/. Public source code at github.com/LanceRoylo/neverranked-outreach. Gemma is open-weight, so any auditor (compliance team, outside reviewer, agency data lead, anyone) can independently re-run the same questions and verify the training-data numbers.

Honest scope and what this does not prove

Four categories is enough to surface a gradient. It is not enough to declare a universal law. The pattern is consistent in direction (commodity-to-relationship is the axis) and in magnitude (the spread is wide enough to matter) but it lives inside a small sample of categories, all in Hawaii, all measured in a single week.

What is not in this teardown:

  • National categories. Everything here is Hawaii-specific. Categories in larger markets may show different patterns because the cohort dynamics differ.
  • Categories outside professional services and consumer financial services. The four measured here cluster narrowly. B2B SaaS, restaurants, real estate brokerages, healthcare specialties beyond dental, all unmeasured here.
  • Whether the pattern holds over time. AI tools refresh their training data and search indices on schedules we do not control. The same categories measured six months from now may produce different numbers.
  • Causation. We measured what AI cites. We did not test whether changing what is on a firm’s site or in third-party publications would cause AI to cite differently. That requires pre-registered experiments against named businesses with control for confounds. Different scope, different methodology.

What an engagement does with this

NeverRanked engagements measure your specific category every week for as long as the engagement runs. For an engagement in a commodity-end category, the punch list weights toward own-site work. For an engagement in a relationship-end category, the punch list weights toward off-site editorial presence. Whether the work moves the needle is a measurement question we keep answering month over month.
