NeverRanked · Teardown 05 · Hawaii wealth management

The lead-gen middlemen sitting between buyers and Hawaii wealth firms.

42-firm cohort, 18 hash-locked questions, 3 usable runs across 2026-05-23 to 2026-05-25. Pattern-readiness cleared. Individual firms anonymized; counts, distributions, and named third-party platforms named.

The headline finding in one sentence: across 42 Hawaii wealth-management firms and 7 AI tools, the top two non-firm sources AI cites are lead-gen platforms (SmartAsset and getwarmer.com, 432 mentions combined) rather than any individual firm. AI is structurally routing wealth-management buyers through matchmaker middlemen before sending them to the firms themselves. For any Hawaii wealth firm, that platform layer is a measurable AEO surface most firms likely do not actively manage.

Why this category matters as a measurement subject

Hawaii wealth management is the relationship-end category in our cross-category teardown. A buyer choosing a wealth manager makes a high-trust, long-horizon decision based on the firm's principals, philosophy, and fit with the buyer's specific scenario (business owner, physician, inherited wealth, etc.). The category's AI-citation surface reflects that: a firm-owned share (47%) just below its third-party share (49%), less firm-dominated than commodity categories like banking. The third-party surface is where the closable competitive ground sits.

Methodology summary

Same 7-AI-tool methodology applied across all NeverRanked teardowns:

  • 5 web-searching AI tools: Perplexity, ChatGPT search, Gemini (grounded with live web search), Microsoft Copilot via Bing organic results, Google AI Overviews.
  • 2 training-data AI tools: Claude (via the Anthropic API), Gemma (open-weight, run on a model-hosting provider so the model itself is independently inspectable).

18 questions a Hawaii wealth-management buyer would actually ask AI, locked at hash ae13579a... so every run compares apples to apples. The actual locked set is published at /methodology/#query-set for review. 3 repetitions per question per AI tool. 3 usable runs spanning 2026-05-23 to 2026-05-25. Pattern-readiness rule of 3 usable runs cleared per the internal pattern-readiness rule.

The 42-firm cohort grew through two passes. 15 anchor firms registered before run 1. 8 additional firms surfaced through run 1 cohort-coverage scan and registered after. The cohort expansion lifted the firm-owned share from 27% on the initial 15-firm cohort to 47% on the full 42-firm cohort, illustrating the cohort-coverage discipline named on the methodology page. National lead-gen platforms and financial-planner directories were deliberately excluded from the cohort; they appear in the data as third-party content sources.

Full methodology, the hash-locked question set, and the dated runs on the claims ledger are at /methodology/ and /claims/.

The structural finding is in the top two rows. SmartAsset (211 mentions) and getwarmer.com (221 mentions) are both lead-gen platforms whose business model is collecting buyer information and routing it to wealth advisors who pay for placement. Combined, they account for 432 of the 3,029 third-party mentions (14% of all non-firm citations). AI is using these platforms as primary sources when answering wealth-management questions for Hawaii.

For a wealth-management firm, this is a real structural fact about the category. If your firm is not represented on SmartAsset and getwarmer.com, you are missing a meaningful share of the AI-citation surface, even before considering Microsoft Copilot or any of the per-firm dynamics. This is also a different shape of surface than the categories below: dental does not have a SmartAsset equivalent; banking does not have a getwarmer.com. Wealth has both because the lead-gen advisor-matchmaker model is a real economic layer in this vertical.

Two rows worth flagging. Microsoft Copilot cites zero firm-owned websites across 787 mentions, the same cohort-wide gap we documented for dental and law. Whichever Hawaii wealth firm ranks first in Bing organic results for the buyer questions effectively owns the Copilot answer while every competitor is invisible there.

The Claude row is worth a closer look. Claude answers from training data and cites firm-owned wealth websites 38% of the time, with the other 62% pulling from third-party content. That is moderate training-data presence: above dental (Claude 34%) and below law firms (Claude 51%, where named-partner editorial content runs deep). For a wealth firm asking "does Claude know my brand," the answer is "Claude names specific firms a fair amount, but still reaches for third-party context more often than not." Hawaii wealth firms sit mid-pack among the relationship-business cohorts in Claude's training data, not at the bottom.

What this means for an individual firm

If you are a Hawaii wealth firm: roughly half of the AI-citation surface for your category is off your own site. Within that off-site share, the largest single block lives on two lead-gen platforms (SmartAsset, getwarmer.com) that most firms probably do not actively manage. The rest is fragmented across category-specific publications, Wikipedia entries for named principals, and Google self-references. The Microsoft Copilot opening is open for every firm in the cohort simultaneously, gated on Bing organic visibility. Claude's training-data layer for Hawaii wealth is moderate, above dental and below law. It is not the bottleneck the lead-gen and Copilot gaps are.

What this teardown does and does not prove

What it does support:

  • The 42-firm cohort is a representative slice of Hawaii wealth-management firms AI tools actually cite for buyer-shaped questions.
  • The 47%/49% firm-owned / third-party split holds across the three usable runs on the full cohort. Run-to-run noise is not driving the headline.
  • The Microsoft Copilot 0% firm-owned share is cohort-wide and consistent across all 3 measurement runs.
  • SmartAsset and getwarmer.com are measurable AEO surfaces specific to this category that most Hawaii wealth firms likely do not actively manage.
  • Claude's training-data presence for Hawaii wealth firms is structurally thinner than for adjacent relationship categories.

What it does not yet support:

  • That presence on SmartAsset or getwarmer.com would cause AI to cite a firm differently. We measured what AI cites. Causation requires pre-registered experiments. Different scope.
  • That Claude's thin Hawaii-wealth training-data presence will close on any specific timeline. Training-data presence is the slowest signal we measure; it forms over years of editorial and brand authority work.
  • That the Microsoft Copilot first-mover opportunity is actually closable for any specific firm. The Bing organic ranking competition is outside what this measurement captures.

Why this is anonymized

None of the 42 firms in this cohort are paying NeverRanked customers. The non-customer anonymization rule applies: counts, distributions, source-type breakdowns, and per-AI-tool numbers are public; individual firm names are not. The pattern is what is informative on a public surface. The named cohort lives only inside paid engagement deliverables, where the named firm is the customer authorizing the use.

One related private artifact exists: a 1:1 brief prepared for Hamada Financial Group as part of an in-person scoping conversation. That brief names the cohort in full and is private to Hamada (noindex, nofollow, takedown on request). The public teardown you are reading is the anonymized version of the same underlying measurement.

A firm that becomes a NeverRanked customer gets a 1:1 deliverable that names every firm in the cohort, names the queries the customer is missing on, and ranks the closable conditions. That deliverable is private to the customer.
