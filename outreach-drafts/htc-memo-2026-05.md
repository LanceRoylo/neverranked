## May 2026: your first baseline memo

Greg,

This is the first of these. It sets the baseline we measure against every month from here. Short version: you are winning the queries that are about you, and you are invisible on a set of queries you should own. The gap is specific and most of it is addressable.

### Where you stand

Across the last 90 days, AI tools cited Hawaii Theatre Center on 940 of 2,346 measured answers, a 40.1 percent citation share. In your registered cohort of 9 Honolulu performing-arts venues, you rank first. The next closest are Blaisdell Center at 287 mentions and Diamond Head Theatre at 281. You are not competing for the top of this category. You hold it.

### What you own

When a buyer's question is about theater, you win it.

- Your brand name and "Hawaii Theatre Center tickets" both sit at 80 percent citation.
- "Historic theater Honolulu" is 80 percent. "Performing arts Honolulu" is 73 percent. "Best theater Honolulu" is 69 percent.

That is the payoff from the schema work. The questions closest to your identity now name you more often than not. Hold that ground. Nothing in the punch list below trades it away.

### Where you are invisible

The pattern in the misses is consistent. You disappear the moment a question stops saying "theater" and starts describing a night out or a use case.

- **Event venue rental Honolulu: 0 percent.** Across 122 measured answers, AI named you zero times. You rent event space. It is on your site. For this query you do not exist to the engines.
- **Things to do downtown Honolulu: 4 percent. Things to do Chinatown Honolulu: 6 percent.** You are a downtown Chinatown landmark and you are absent from the "what should I do here" answer.
- **Live music venue Honolulu: 4 percent. Honolulu nightlife shows: 3 percent.** You host live music and evening shows. The engines do not connect you to those framings.
- **Family friendly shows for a 7 year old: 15 percent.** You program family work. This one is closer but still mostly missed.

None of these are about your brand strength. They are about the engines not associating you with the adjacent, compositional ways people actually ask. That association is built with content that names the use case, not just the venue.

### The punch list, in priority order

Prioritization lives here, in this memo, by design. Atlas will not rank these for you between memos. This is the order I would work them.

1. **Event rental page, written for the query.** You have the asset and a 0 percent result. This is the highest return move on the board. One page that says, in plain language, that Hawaii Theatre Center rents its space for events, what kinds, capacity, how to inquire. The goal is to exist at all for "event venue rental Honolulu," where you currently do not.

2. **One "things to do in downtown and Chinatown" anchor.** A page or article that places HTC inside the downtown Chinatown experience, not as a standalone venue. This is the lever for the two things-to-do queries and it reinforces the nightlife and live-music framings at the same time.

3. **Name the use cases you already serve.** Live music, evening shows, family programming. Where your existing pages describe events as theater, add the language a buyer uses: a live music venue, a night out, a family-friendly show. Small edits, aimed at the framings above.

Your team or your agency does this work. I measure what moves.

### One engine note

You are strong on Gemma, Gemini, and Perplexity, all above 54 percent. You are effectively absent on Microsoft Copilot, which runs on Bing, at 1.6 percent. Copilot weights its own index differently than the others. I am flagging it, not prescribing for it yet. I want another month of data before I say whether that is a fixable gap or just how Copilot behaves in your category.

### What I am watching next month

- Whether the event rental query moves off zero once a page exists.
- Whether the downtown Chinatown framing lifts the cluster of adjacent queries together, which is the pattern I would expect if the association is the real lever.
- Copilot, for a second read.

You can ask Atlas about any of the numbers above between now and the next memo. It will answer what the data shows. It will not tell you what to do. That part is mine, and it is in this memo.
