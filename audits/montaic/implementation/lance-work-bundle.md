## CRITICAL — Read this before publishing A11

The A11 Fair Housing article draft has a factual problem I caught during citation verification. Fix this before publish or the article's credibility collapses the first time a compliance attorney fact-checks it.

### What the draft claims

From `A11-fair-housing-pillar-article.md`, paragraph 2 of the body:

> "In May 2024, the Department of Housing and Urban Development published guidance titled 'Application of the Fair Housing Act's Guarantee of Non-Discrimination in Housing-Related Transactions Based on the Use of Artificial Intelligence.'"

And further down:

> "The 2024 guidance extends this in two ways: First, it explicitly confirms that AI-generated content is held to the same standard as human-written content."

### What HUD actually published

HUD did release two AI-related Fair Housing guidance documents on May 2, 2024 (press release HUD No. 24-098). But their scope is NOT AI-generated listing content. The two documents are:

1. **FHEO Guidance on Application of the Fair Housing Act to the Screening of Applicants for Rental Housing** — about AI tenant screening tools, not content.
2. **FHEO Guidance on Application of the Fair Housing Act to the Advertising of Housing, Credit, and Other Real Estate-Related Transactions through Digital Platforms** — about algorithmic ad targeting (who sees a housing ad), not ad content generation (what the ad says).

Neither document specifically addresses ChatGPT-style AI-generated listing copy. The draft's central citation is inaccurate.

### Why this matters

- A compliance attorney or trade publication editor will fact-check this in under 5 minutes.
- If the article misrepresents HUD guidance, Montaic's credibility on the Fair Housing compliance claim (the entire product positioning) takes collateral damage.
- The article's underlying thesis is still correct. Agents ARE legally responsible for AI-generated listing content. But not because of 2024 HUD guidance. Because of 42 U.S.C. § 3604(c), which has been in force since 1968 and applies to "any notice, statement, or advertisement" regardless of who or what wrote it.

### Recommended rewrite

Replace the opening framing. Instead of "HUD issued guidance in 2024 confirming AI is covered," use "the Fair Housing Act already covers AI content — and HUD's 2024 AI guidance makes clear the agency is watching this space."

Three specific paragraph-level edits:

**Current paragraph 2 (delete):**
> In April 2024, HUD quietly issued guidance confirming that the Fair Housing Act applies to AI-generated real estate advertising and listing content.

**Replacement:**
> The Fair Housing Act has always applied to AI-generated listing content. The statute at 42 U.S.C. § 3604(c) prohibits any advertisement that indicates preference or discrimination based on a protected characteristic, regardless of who or what produced it. And in May 2024, HUD signaled it is actively watching the AI space when it issued two separate guidance documents on algorithmic tenant screening and algorithmic ad targeting. Neither document specifically addressed AI-generated content, but the direction of travel is obvious.

**Current paragraph 3 opening (delete):**
> In May 2024, the Department of Housing and Urban Development published guidance titled "Application of the Fair Housing Act's Guarantee of Non-Discrimination in Housing-Related Transactions Based on the Use of Artificial Intelligence."

**Replacement:**
> The Fair Housing Act at 42 U.S.C. § 3604(c) has prohibited discriminatory housing advertising since 1968. Its regulations at 24 C.F.R. § 100.75 make clear the prohibition covers "any notice, statement or advertisement" across "applications, flyers, brochures, deeds, signs, banners, posters, billboards or any documents used with respect to the sale or rental of a dwelling." Courts have consistently interpreted this to mean what matters is how an "ordinary reader" perceives the advertisement, not what the publisher intended. There is no exemption for content generated by a machine.

**Current paragraph 4 (delete):**
> The 2024 guidance extends this in two ways: First, it explicitly confirms that AI-generated content is held to the same standard as human-written content.

**Replacement:**
> Two things are worth understanding. First, the statutory standard for discriminatory advertising is what the content says, not who wrote it. The agent who publishes the advertisement is the publisher under the law, which means the agent is responsible for the content regardless of whether a human, a generic AI tool, or a specialized tool produced the first draft. Second, liability attaches under a "disparate impact" standard, not just "intentional discrimination." Even if neither the agent nor the AI intended to discriminate, content that has a discriminatory effect on a protected class can still violate the Act.

The rest of the article is fine as drafted. The five violation patterns, the workflow recommendations, the FAQs — all of that holds. Only the framing of HUD's role needs correcting.
