### The problem the piece describes

A14 published three-dataset numbers. In the A14 draft, Section 3 contains this line:

"Across 53 listings, ChatGPT opened 96 percent of them with [the same two-word phrase]. Fifty-one out of fifty-three."

That number sat inside a paragraph and did most of its work implicitly. Nobody reading A14 quickly would come away naming the figure as a takeaway. A16 pulls it out and makes it the headline, then pairs it with a second finding that Phase 2 re-mining surfaced and that A14 did not name at all.

**Two linked findings.** The first finding is the 96 percent opening. Fifty-one out of 53 ChatGPT outputs began with the same two-word phrase. The second finding is the score-distribution collapse. All 53 ChatGPT outputs landed in the 3-4 bucket on the Montaic grader. Not a single output cleared 5/10. Zero listings above mediocre in the full corpus.

The two findings are the same story told twice. The 96 percent opening is the visible symptom. The zero-ceiling score is the downstream consequence.

**The transparency note the piece owes the reader.** One of these facts you can verify yourself. The 96 percent figure is a phrase-count check anyone can run by reading the 53 ChatGPT outputs and grepping the first two words. The zero-ceiling figure depends on the Montaic grader's scoring methodology, which is proprietary. The piece states the distinction directly in the opening paragraph: one fact is objectively checkable, one requires trusting the grader. Both tell the same story. Transparency about verifiability is how the piece earns its authority on the second finding without appearing to grade its own homework in silence.

**The mechanism.** ChatGPT, given 53 different properties with 53 different feature sets in 53 different Nashville neighborhoods, produced 51 outputs that began with the same two-word phrase and 53 outputs that scored identically. That is not a coincidence. That is the training distribution collapsing on itself. The model was trained on millions of real estate listings, the most common opening in the corpus is the same two-word phrase, and the model defaults to the most common opening regardless of the specific property. Because the default opening is generic, and because the specificity that follows is also defaulted toward the training average, the whole output compresses toward a single quality bucket.

**The score distribution compared across tools.** Zillow originals: one listing in the 7-8 bucket, 20 in the 5-6 bucket, 31 in the 3-4 bucket, one in the 1-2 bucket. ChatGPT: zero in any bucket above 3-4. Montaic default: two in the 7-8 bucket, 30 in the 5-6 bucket, 21 in the 3-4 bucket. The distribution shapes are different. Zillow is bimodal around the mediocre-to-decent range with one outlier. Montaic is right-skewed toward 5-6. ChatGPT is a single point mass at 3-4. That single point mass is the core finding of A16.

**The three outliers.** Three listings across 159 total (53 per dataset, 159 in aggregate) scored 7/10 or higher. None of them came from ChatGPT. Two came from the Montaic default path (the Brentwood Pella window listing and the Stone Oak Builders Radnor Lake listing). One came from the Zillow original corpus (a downtown Nashville views listing). Those three outliers are the piece's positive examples. What they share with each other, and do not share with the 156 lower-scoring outputs, is what A16 calls the three-sentence rule.

**The practical effect.** Every agent currently using ChatGPT for listings is publishing descriptions that open identically and cap at mediocre. Buyers who see multiple listings in a day see the same opening over and over. The distinctiveness of the property evaporates in the first sentence, and nothing in the body recovers it.

The piece names the phrase, publishes the exact count (51 out of 53), publishes the score distribution (all 53 ChatGPT outputs at 3-4, zero above), walks the five or six other top overused patterns with their counts ("stunning" in 81 percent, "spacious" in 81 percent, "dream home" in 68 percent, "boasts" in 66 percent, "beautiful" in 64 percent), and shows what a 7/10 listing looks like by comparing all three of the high-scoring outputs against the ChatGPT baseline.

### The Montaic bridge

Two bridges. The commercial bridge is the three-way comparison on the two-word opening phrase. Zillow originals: 9 percent (5 of 53). ChatGPT: 96 percent (51 of 53). Montaic default: 13 percent (7 of 53). Montaic is roughly seven times less likely to produce the default opening than ChatGPT is, and close to the Zillow baseline. The mechanism argument is not that Montaic is immune to the collapse but that Montaic's fact-surfacing layer runs before the writing layer. Specific facts are present in the context when the writing layer begins, which makes it more likely that a specific fact lands by sentence two or three. A tool that surfaces facts first does not have to collapse onto the most common phrase because it has actual material to put in the body.

The score evidence: two of the three 7/10 listings across all 159 came from Montaic default. Both Montaic 7/10s open with the template word "Exceptional" and land a specific fact by sentence two (Pella windows at $45K, Stone Oak Builders at 2022 on 1.28 acres). That is the three-sentence rule earning the score despite a generic opening word.

The product bridge is the voice-locked path. A16 does not run the voice-locked experiment (that is A17's frame, Candidate D deferred). A16 sets it up. The closing section can name "the 13 percent Montaic figure is the default path without any style sample. When a voice sample is uploaded, the default opening patterns get replaced by the specific patterns of the sample, and we expect the score distribution to widen further. The voice-locked experiment is the next piece."

Product bridge is natural. Commercial bridge is proven by A14 data and sharpened by the Phase 2 score-distribution finding.

**Outlier 2. The first word is a template word ("Exceptional") but the rest of the sentence lands four specific facts: named builder, build year, lot size, named neighborhood. **Outlier 3. This three-bedroom, two-bathroom home features $45,000 in Pella windows and doors with transferable warranty..." Sentence one is generic. Sentence two lands a specific, dollar-denominated, branded fact. The three-sentence rule is satisfied in sentence two.

**The pattern the three outliers share.** By the end of sentence three, each of them names at least one fact that only applies to that specific property. A dollar amount, a named brand, a measurement, a year, a confirmed benefit. The 156 lower-scoring listings across all three datasets do not do this by sentence three. ChatGPT's outputs, specifically, do not do it anywhere in the first paragraph. The three-sentence rule is the variable that separates the outliers from the baseline.

**Why the rule is actionable.** It does not demand the agent produce a clever opening line. It does not demand voice-locked infrastructure or fact-surfacing tooling. It demands only that the agent audit the first three sentences and verify that at least one specific, property-unique fact has landed. If sentence three is vague, rewrite. That is the Monday-morning change the piece sells.

A16 uses all three outliers in the "What a 7/10 looks like" section. The comparison is explicit: ChatGPT's default opening (96 percent of outputs) versus the three outlier openings. One paragraph per outlier. Specific, concrete, copyable. The Brentwood listing is still the hero among the three because it has the strongest single fact ($45,000 Pella windows with transferable warranty) and the grader feedback is on file, but it is not the only positive example and it is not an example of a great opening line. It is an example of body specificity rescuing a generic opening.

### Blocker 3: External citation audit for opening-phrase research. CLOSED 2026-04-11.

**Actual effort:** 40 minutes.

**Findings: new citations added to the A13/A14 shelf.**

1. **Shumailov et al., "AI models collapse when trained on recursively generated data," Nature (2024).** PubMed: pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39048682/. Establishes the theoretical mechanism for why ChatGPT's 96 percent opening phrase happens: training distribution narrowing, tails vanishing first, outputs drifting toward bland central tendencies. This is the cleanest theoretical anchor for the mode-collapse argument A16 is making. Already the most-cited model-collapse paper and will stay that way through 2026.

2. **"Strong Model Collapse," ICLR 2025 conference paper.** OpenReview: openreview.net/forum?id=et5l9qPUhm. Confirms the Shumailov finding holds under a range of training conditions and that even a small fraction of synthetic data (as little as 1 per 1000) produces collapse. A16 uses this to answer the "but GPT-4o-mini is trained on real data" objection: even a trace of the MLS boilerplate corpus in the training mix is enough to lock in the collapse on this specific vertical.

3. **"Attributing Mode Collapse in the Fine-Tuning of Large Language Models," OpenReview 2025.** URL: openreview.net/forum?id=3pDMYjpOxk. Specific to RLHF-style fine-tuning, which is what ChatGPT consumer-facing models are under. Relevant if the piece wants to argue that the opening-phrase collapse is not just a pretraining artifact but is reinforced by the RLHF feedback loop that rewards friendly, greeting-style openings in general and the two-word opener specifically in listing contexts.

**Findings: citations already on the A13/A14 shelf that A16 reuses.**

4. **Zillow Research, "Listing Language: From the Bottom of the Bubble to Now" (2016).** zillow.com/research/comparing-listing-language-12431. The 6-million-listing phrase-frequency study that A13 and A14 both cite. Establishes the precedent for this kind of corpus analysis and provides the historical baseline for phrase frequencies before the GPT era.

5. **Zillow Research, "Lucrative Listing Descriptions" (2018).** zillow.com/research/listing-descriptions-sales-2018-19285. 3.6-million-sale study showing listings mentioning specific features ("steam shower," "professional appliance") earned 29 percent higher sale prices. A16 uses this as the dollar-denominated evidence that specificity converts. It is the commercial-impact citation that backs the three-sentence rule without depending on the Montaic grader.

6. **"Verbalized Sampling: How to Mitigate Mode Collapse and Unlock LLM Diversity" (arXiv, October 2025).** arxiv.org/html/2510.01171v1. Already cited in A13 and A14. Stays on the A16 shelf because it is the mitigation-path citation: it names what a tool would have to do differently to avoid the collapse, which is the implicit argument Montaic makes with its fact-surfacing layer.

7. **NAR Profile of Home Buyers (rolling annual).** 51 percent of all buyers find the home they purchase online. A16 cites this once as the "opening sentence matters because buyers are skimming online" anchor. Already on the A14 shelf.

**Gap identified (and named in the piece).** No academic or industry study specifically tests whether the opening sentence of a real estate listing produces more engagement than later sentences. Scroll-depth studies on listing pages exist but are proprietary to platforms (Zillow, Realtor.com) and not publicly benchmarked. A16 acknowledges the gap in one sentence and grounds the three-sentence rule in the Montaic grader data plus the general attention-decay literature rather than a listing-specific scroll study. The piece's credibility on the "opening matters" argument rests on the 159-listing corpus itself, not on borrowed authority.
