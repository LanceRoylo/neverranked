### The problem the piece describes

A14 published three-dataset numbers. In the A14 draft, Section 3 contains this line:

"Across 53 listings, ChatGPT opened 96 percent of them with [the same two-word phrase]. Fifty-one out of fifty-three."

That number sat inside a paragraph and did most of its work implicitly. Nobody reading A14 quickly would come away naming the figure as a takeaway. A16 pulls it out and makes it the headline.

The deeper pattern is what the figure reveals. ChatGPT, given 53 different properties with 53 different feature sets in 53 different Nashville neighborhoods, produced 51 outputs that began with the same two-word phrase. That is not a coincidence. That is the training distribution collapsing on itself. The model was trained on millions of real estate listings, the most common opening in the corpus is the same two-word phrase, and the model defaults to the most common opening regardless of the specific property.

The practical effect is that every agent currently using ChatGPT for listings is publishing descriptions that open identically. Buyers who see multiple listings in a day see the same opening over and over. The distinctiveness of the property evaporates in the first sentence. Whatever specific detail the agent wanted to lead with got replaced by the statistical average.

The piece names the phrase, publishes the exact count (51 out of 53), walks the five or six other top overused patterns with their counts ("stunning" in 81 percent, "spacious" in 81 percent, "dream home" in 68 percent, "boasts" in 66 percent, the literal phrase "MLS Description:" appearing in 6 outputs), and shows what a non-default opening looks like using the $45,000 window Brentwood listing as the positive example.

### The Montaic bridge

Two bridges. The commercial bridge is the same as A14: Montaic default was the only tool in the three-way comparison that did not produce this pattern (the two-word opening phrase appears in 13 percent of Montaic outputs versus 96 percent of ChatGPT outputs). The mechanism argument is that Montaic's compliance layer and style layer were not built against the average of the MLS corpus. They were built against specific rules and specific voice samples. A tool that checks phrases against rules does not collapse onto the most common phrase.

The product bridge is the voice-locked path. A16 does not run the voice-locked experiment (that is A17's frame, Candidate D deferred). A16 sets it up. The closing section can name "the 13 percent Montaic figure is the default path without any style sample. When a voice sample is uploaded, the default opening patterns get replaced by the specific patterns of the sample. The voice-locked experiment is the next piece."

Product bridge is natural. Commercial bridge is already proven by A14 data.

### Absorbed Candidate E: the $45,000 Brentwood window listing

Candidate E from the A16 rescoring block ("The $45,000 Window Listing" case study) absorbs into A16 as a body section. Rationale: a single-property case study is too narrow to carry a full pillar, but it is the perfect positive example for a "here is what a non-default opening looks like" section inside A16.

The Brentwood listing scored 7 out of 10 (the highest Montaic score in the Nashville 53 run). Its opening led with a dollar figure ("$45,000 in Pella windows and doors with transferable warranty") rather than a template adjective. The grader rewarded the opening for naming a specific investment with buyer-actionable financial context.

A16 uses the Brentwood listing in the "What to write instead" section. The contrast is explicit: ChatGPT's default opening versus the specific dollar-figure opening that beat both ChatGPT and the Zillow original. One paragraph per pattern. Specific, concrete, copyable.
