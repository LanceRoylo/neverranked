### The problem the piece describes

Between April 2025 and early 2027, four overlapping federal actions are reshaping what Fair Housing enforcement looks like for real estate advertising:

1. **EO 14281 (April 23, 2025):** established policy to eliminate disparate impact liability "to the maximum degree possible." Still active. Sets the interpretive direction for everything downstream.

2. **FHEO enforcement priorities memo (September 16, 2025):** de-prioritized disparate impact investigations. Prioritized "facially discriminatory conduct." Issued one day before the Gibbs withdrawal memo. Creates the enforcement posture agents are operating under right now.

3. **HUD proposed rule on disparate impact (January 14, 2026):** would eliminate 24 C.F.R. Part 100, Subpart G (the Discriminatory Effects standard) and remove the language that discriminatory effects can establish an FHA violation without discriminatory intent. Comment period closed February 13, 2026. Not yet finalized. If finalized, this is the most significant structural change to Fair Housing advertising enforcement since the 2013 rule that created the standard.

4. **DOJ v. Meta settlement sunset (June 27, 2026):** the court-monitored compliance agreement from the 2022 discriminatory ad targeting settlement expires. This is the single most relevant enforcement precedent for digital platforms and AI-driven ad systems. When it sunsets, the question becomes whether DOJ pursues renewal, lets it expire, or files new action. A15 documented that this settlement was active during the entire 201-day guidance withdrawal gap.

No single article currently maps all four events on one timeline with the practical implications for listing descriptions. Trade press covers each event individually. A18 puts them on one page with the "what it means for your listings" translation for each date.

### Montaic bridge

Montaic's compliance layer screens against 42 U.S.C. 3604(c), 24 C.F.R. 100.75, and a case law corpus. A15 established that Montaic's compliance did not change when HUD withdrew guidance because Montaic never cited guidance as authority. The same argument holds through A18's calendar: the events on the calendar change the enforcement posture and the regulatory framework, but the statute and the case law that Montaic screens against remain intact. The bridge is "Montaic's compliance layer is anchored to the statute, not the guidance, so the calendar changes the landscape but not our screening."

### Named limitation

The piece is a calendar, not a prediction. It names what is on the public record. It does not predict whether the proposed rule will be finalized, whether DOJ will renew the Meta settlement, or whether FHEO will reverse its enforcement priorities. The honest note section will name this directly: "This is a map of what is scheduled. The territory will change."

## Phase 3: Draft (NOT STARTED, DEFERRED TO JULY 2026)

### Target length

2000 to 2600 words. Same range as A13 through A17.

### Working structure (8 body sections plus FAQ plus closing)

1. **Opening hook.** Lead with the DOJ v. Meta sunset date (June 27, 2026) as the sharpest near-term event. Name the four overlapping actions. Promise a dated calendar, not an opinion piece.
2. **The four events on the calendar.** One subsection per event. Date, document name, Federal Register or docket citation, current status, and what it means for listing descriptions. This is the reference section agents will bookmark.
3. **The timeline view.** A chronological sequence from EO 14281 (April 2025) through the proposed rule finalization window (2026-2027). Visual or list format showing the sequence of narrowing.
4. **What changed and what did not.** A15 callback: the statute (42 U.S.C. 3604(c)) and the regulation (24 C.F.R. 100.75) are unchanged. The enforcement posture shifted. The guidance layer thinned. The proposed rule, if finalized, would remove the disparate impact regulatory standard. Walk agents through which layer protects them and which layer is moving.
5. **What this means for AI-generated listings.** The DOJ v. Meta settlement specifically targeted algorithmic ad targeting. The proposed rule addresses disparate impact. AI listing generators that produce discriminatory output could face enforcement under the statute even if the regulatory standard narrows. Montaic bridge: screens against the statute, not the guidance.
6. **The honest note.** Calendar, not prediction. Names what is on the public record. Does not predict finalization timing, DOJ renewal decisions, or enforcement direction changes.
7. **What agents should do now.** Three actions: (a) audit current listings against 42 U.S.C. 3604(c), which is not moving, (b) watch the DOJ v. Meta sunset for renewal or expiration, (c) if using AI tools, confirm the tool screens against statute and case law, not just guidance.
8. **Closing.** The four-pillar Fair Housing arc: A11 framed the AI compliance question, A12 named the words you cannot use, A15 mapped the guidance withdrawal, A18 put the enforcement calendar on one page.
9. **FAQ.** Six Q&A pairs for FAQPage schema.
