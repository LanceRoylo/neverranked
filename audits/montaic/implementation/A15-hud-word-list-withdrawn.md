### The problem the piece describes

Compliance training still hands out a word list derived from a 1989 HUD memorandum. That memo was a staff advisory, not a rule. It never had statutory authority. It was circulated widely because nothing else existed in plain-language form. At some point in the 2000s it stopped appearing on hud.gov. Nothing formally replaced it.

The practical effect is that agents learn a list of banned phrases with no understanding of which statute, regulation, or case the phrase is banned under. When the list misses a new phrase or over-flags an old one, the agent has no framework to reason about the gap. They either publish risky content they should not publish or they avoid safe content they could publish.

The piece walks through four things:

1. Where the 1989 list came from.
2. Why it was withdrawn or stopped being maintained.
3. What actually governs Fair Housing listing language today.
4. A short list of still-dangerous phrases the 1989 list did not flag.

### The Montaic bridge

Montaic screens every generated listing against 42 U.S.C. § 3604(c), 24 C.F.R. § 100.75, and a case law corpus maintained and updated as new decisions land. The product bridge is not "we use the 1989 list better than everyone else." It is "we do not use the 1989 list at all because it is not the authority, and here is what we use instead."

The bridge is built into the structural claim of the piece. The piece does not argue that agents need a better phrase list. It argues that agents need a framework that evolves with case law, and the framework is what Montaic ships.

## Phase 2: Research (OPEN)

### Blocker 1: 1989 memo provenance. OPEN.

**Effort:** 2 to 3 hours.

Confirm the exact date and HUD office that circulated the 1989 memo. A12 cited "1989 HUD memorandum" without full attribution. A15 needs the exact citation (office name, memo identifier if any, circulation date) or a clear statement that no formal citation exists because the memo was staff-level guidance.

**Approach:**
- Search hud.gov archives via Wayback Machine for historical references.
- Check law review articles on Fair Housing advertising for secondary citations.
- Check FHEO public records for any direct reference.
- Check the Stolzoff-Levin 1990s Fair Housing advertising guidance papers for the earliest secondary source citation.

**Fallback:** if no formal citation exists, the piece names the absence explicitly. "The memo is cited in hundreds of training materials without a single consistent attribution. That is part of the problem." The absence of provenance becomes part of the argument instead of a blocker.

### Blocker 2: Case law selection. OPEN.

**Effort:** 3 to 4 hours.

The piece needs two or three post-2010 Fair Housing advertising cases that show (a) a phrase the 1989 list did not flag but the statute did, or (b) a phrase the 1989 list over-flagged that later case law cleared. That is the proof that the list is stale in both directions.

**Approach:**
- Check HUD case law database for post-2010 advertising enforcement.
- Check DOJ Fair Housing enforcement actions 2015-2025.
- Check state commission enforcement records for NY, CA, MA (high-volume states).
- Target: two cases that cleanly show the stale-list problem. Three maximum.

**Risk:** if the cases do not cleanly show what the piece needs, the frame softens to "the 1989 list has not been updated in 35 years" without the enforcement proof. That weakens the piece but does not kill it.
