1. The headline finding

For Hawaii wealth-management queries a prospect might type into ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, Microsoft Copilot, Google AI Overviews, Claude, or Gemma — "best wealth manager in Hawaii," "fiduciary financial advisor Honolulu," "Hawaii wealth manager for business owner," and 15 similar — Hamada Financial Group appears in 8 AI citations across the full 18-query × 7-engine measurement.

7 of those 8 citations came from a single query: "Hamada Financial vs Cadinha vs CKW" — the only query in the set that named Hamada explicitly.

On the 17 demand-shape queries (the queries where a prospect is asking AI to find a Hawaii wealth manager without already knowing your name), Hamada was not cited by any AI engine.

That is the structural problem this measurement surfaces. It is also the closable one.

The cross-category finding worth naming. NeverRanked also published a teardown on Hawaii consumer banking last week (/teardowns/bank-honolulu/). The two categories produce dramatically different AI citation patterns:

  • Consumer banking: 53% of citations go to bank-owned sites, 42% to third-party. Banks own their AI presence directly.
  • Wealth management: 27% to firm-owned sites, 70% to third-party. AI cites third-party content about wealth managers far more than the firms' own sites.

For Hamada specifically: the playbook that works for Bank of Hawaii (own-site optimization) is not the playbook that works for Hamada. The work for wealth-management AI presence happens largely off-site — in the editorial and authority surfaces AI engines actually pull from.
