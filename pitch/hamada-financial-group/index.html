1. The headline finding

We asked all 7 AI tools the kinds of questions a Hawaii wealth-management buyer would actually ask. Things like "best wealth manager in Hawaii," "fiduciary financial advisor Honolulu," "Hawaii wealth manager for business owner," and 15 similar. We ran each question across ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, Microsoft Copilot, Google AI Overviews, Claude, and Gemma.

Across that full 18-question set, Hamada Financial Group appears in 8 mentions.

7 of those 8 mentions came from a single question: "Hamada Financial vs Cadinha vs CKW." That was the only question in the set that named Hamada by name.

On the other 17 questions, the ones where a buyer is searching for a Hawaii wealth manager without knowing your name yet, Hamada was not mentioned by any AI tool.

That is the real problem this measurement surfaces. It is also the one that can be closed.

6. What “better” looks like, observably

NeverRanked does not promise that AI will start mentioning you more. We measure. But the data above tells you what the firms AI cites consistently are doing differently, and what would have to change for Hamada to share those conditions. Three concrete paths.

Path 1. Show up on the open-ended questions

Today: Hamada is mentioned on 0 of the 17 open-ended questions. What “better” looks like: Hamada is mentioned on 5 to 10 of those 17 within the next 60 to 90 days of consistent editorial work.

What the firms mentioned there are observably doing:

  • masudalehrman.com (mentioned 59 times, usually first) has a published practice-area page with a clear FAQ for every category the questions name (retirement planning, business owners, real estate investors, inherited wealth). Each page targets one buyer with a first-person founder narrative, the service description, and the named buyer type.
  • fphawaii.com (mentioned 43 times, usually first) appears in third-party “best Hawaii wealth manager” lists in local publications (Hawaii Business Magazine, Star-Advertiser business section). Those editorial mentions are what AI surfaces, not their homepage.
  • creativeplanning.com (mentioned 30 times) is on most national fiduciary-advisor directory lists. AI mentions those directory listings as the trust signal, not the firm’s own site.

What the engagement measures: month-over-month change in how many of the 18 questions mention Hamada, how many AI tools mention Hamada per question, and where in the answer your name shows up. Movement is observable in 4 to 8 weeks for the first 2 or 3 questions you close.

Path 2. Get into Claude and Gemma’s training data

Today: Claude and Gemma mention Hamada zero times across the measurement. These AI tools answer from their training data, not from live search. So a buyer who asks Claude “should I work with Hamada Financial Group?” gets nothing, because Claude has no memory of Hamada.

What “better” looks like: Hamada is mentioned in 5 to 15 Claude or Gemma responses per measurement when the firm name is in the question, showing that training-data presence has formed.

What the firms mentioned there are observably doing:

  • Wikipedia entries for the firm or its named founders. The Hochuli Group at Merrill (mentioned 8 times on Claude) has a long-standing presence in financial press coverage.
  • Named-firm mentions in published news media (Pacific Business News, Hawaii Business Magazine, CNBC FA 100 listing for Cadinha & Co specifically). Cadinha’s CNBC FA 100 ranking is the kind of editorial source AI tools have demonstrably learned to weight in this category.
  • Sustained named-founder content (op-eds, contributed articles, conference talks transcribed to web). The AI training-data layer compounds slowly. This is a 6 to 12 month signal, not a 30-day one.

What the engagement measures: Claude and Gemma mention count for Hamada quarter-over-quarter. This is the slowest signal but the most defensible once it forms.

Path 3. Show up in Microsoft Copilot (the gap everyone has)

Today: Microsoft Copilot mentions zero Hawaii wealth-management firms in the data. Every firm in this category is invisible there. The closable advantage: whichever firm shows up first in Bing search results for these questions effectively owns the Copilot answer while every competitor is still invisible.

What “better” looks like: Hamada appears in Microsoft Copilot for 3 to 5 of the 17 open-ended questions within 90 days, ahead of any other Hawaii wealth manager.

What that requires:

  • Bing organic search visibility for the named questions. The current Bing top 5 for these questions is dominated by national publications (SmartAsset, Investopedia, Forbes), not Hawaii-specific firm content.
  • Hamada-authored or Hamada-cited content on those national publications, indexed by Bing, ranked in the top 5 for at least the most common questions.
  • A first-mover advantage: this gap is open for every Hawaii wealth manager. The firm that closes it first owns the Copilot answer until others catch up.

What the engagement measures: Hamada appearances on Microsoft Copilot per measurement window, broken out by question. We also catch the moment Bing starts ranking a Hamada-relevant URL in the top 5.

The honest framing

None of the above is a promise. NeverRanked measures observable conditions. Whether Hamada’s work produces the measurable change depends on what work Hamada actually ships, the quality of it, and how AI tools weight the resulting signals, which we do not control. What we do is name the specific work the data points at, measure whether it lands, and tell you honestly if it doesn’t. The competitors above are doing observable things that correlate with their mention patterns. Whether replicating those things replicates the outcome is a measurement question, not a guarantee.

7. Why this is monthly, not a one-time audit

The work splits into two parts.

Initial discovery (the $4,500 kickoff, 3 weeks). We lock the 18 questions a Hawaii wealth-management buyer would actually ask AI. We run them across all 7 AI tools daily for three weeks. We build the cohort baseline. We hand Hamada a research memo and a clear list of what to fix. This is the only part of the engagement that produces a single, finished deliverable.

Long-term surfacing (the $1,500/month, ongoing). AI answers shift constantly. New questions emerge as the category matures. Buyers are asking AI things in 2026 they weren’t asking last year. Competitors move. AI training data refreshes. AI tools roll out new versions that weight different signals. The monthly engagement re-measures every week, catches the moment something moves, surfaces new questions worth tracking, and reports honestly whether the work the kickoff prescribed is landing.

A one-time AI audit ages out in 60 to 90 days. The kickoff is where you start. The monthly is how you stay current.
