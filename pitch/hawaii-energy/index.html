02 The six things AI engines can't see

Each gap below maps to a specific class of resident or business query that Hawaii Energy is probably losing today.

Gap 01

No Organization or NonprofitOrganization node.

When ChatGPT, Gemini, or Perplexity is asked "who runs Hawaii's energy efficiency rebate program" or "what is Hawaii Energy," the engines need a structured Organization (or NonprofitOrganization) node to answer authoritatively. Right now there isn't one. The site says "Hawaii Energy" in the title bar and that is roughly all the engines can extract about the entity. The fix is a single JSON-LD block at the root with legal name, alternate name, founder/operator (Leidos), funder reference (Hawaii Public Utilities Commission), description, areaServed (Hawaii state), and full contact details. This is the foundational identity fix that everything else hangs on.

Gap 02

No Service schema for any rebate program.

When someone asks "how do I get a rebate for a heat pump water heater in Hawaii" or "what HVAC rebates are available on Oahu," the AI engines look for Service nodes that match the query intent. Hawaii Energy publishes pages for HVAC rebates, water-heating rebates, lighting rebates, and the business rebate suite. None are structured as Service nodes. Each program should be a Service node with provider (Hawaii Energy), areaServed, serviceType, audience (residential or business), and offers (rebate amount or range). Done correctly, this turns hawaiienergy.com from a page that AI engines summarize into a set of programs they can cite directly by name.

Gap 03

No HowTo schema for application steps.

The site walks visitors through how to apply for various rebates in plain prose. Useful for humans, invisible to AI engines as structured guidance. Each application process (residential rebate, business rebate, Energy Advantage Program enrollment, Clean Energy Allies sign-up) should be a HowTo node with steps, supplies, and estimated time. This unlocks "how do I apply for [rebate] in Hawaii" queries where the AI cites a step-by-step from your site instead of inferring one or pointing to a contractor.

Gap 04

No FAQPage schema on rebate or program pages.

Every rebate page contains the obvious questions: who qualifies, how much is the rebate, what equipment is eligible, how long does the application take, what documentation is needed. Right now those questions are answered as prose paragraphs. As FAQ nodes, each Q&A becomes a directly citable answer that AI engines can quote verbatim. FAQ schema is the single highest-yield schema type for educational content based on the 730-citation study we run our roadmaps from.

Gap 05

No Person schema for the team page.

/about/our-team/ exists with named leadership and program managers. To AI engines, those names are unstructured strings on a page. Person schema with name, jobTitle, worksFor (Hawaii Energy), and sameAs (LinkedIn URL) lets AI engines disambiguate authorship and authority. When a journalist or researcher asks "who runs Hawaii's residential rebate program," the engines should be able to name the right person. Right now they can't.

Gap 06

No Article schema on success stories or energy-saving tips.

/about/success-stories/ and /for-homes/tips-to-save-energy/ are real editorial content. Both should ship as Article schema with author, publisher, datePublished, image, and headline. Without it, the engines can quote the prose but cannot cite the article as an authored, dated piece, which is what makes a citation count toward authority signals. The 18-point partial-schema penalty applies hardest to editorial content, which is why this is on the list even though it is the lowest-effort fix.
