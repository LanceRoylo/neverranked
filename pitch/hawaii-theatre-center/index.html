3. Where Hawaii Theatre is missing

The clearest gap is Microsoft Copilot at 0%. Copilot and ChatGPT search both draw heavily on Microsoft’s Bing index, and they are your two lowest engines (0% and 7%) while the others sit at 9% to 14%. That pattern points to a Bing-side visibility gap rather than a content problem, which is good news, because it is a fixable technical opening rather than a reputation one. The punch list turns it into a specific action.

4. What AI gets wrong about Hawaii Theatre

We checked both brand queries (“Hawaii Theatre history” and “Hawaii Theatre Center upcoming events”) across all seven engines and all three runs.

Your history is accurate everywhere it is told. ChatGPT search, Perplexity, Gemini, and Claude all correctly report the 1922 opening, the “Pride of the Pacific” name, the original architects, and the 1984 closure followed by the 1996 restoration. No corrections needed on the historical record.

Your current events held up too, which is rarer. The four search-based engines read your live events page and reported real upcoming shows (Steven Ho on June 13, Miss Hawaii on June 18 to 19, the Rainbow Film Festival in July) with dates that matched across engines. The two training-data engines, Claude and Gemma, correctly declined to invent specific dates and pointed to your website instead. We did not find a fabricated event or an incorrect date in this month’s responses.

Two real errors are worth fixing:

  1. Microsoft Copilot does not recognize Hawaii Theatre as a subject at all. Across all eighteen brand responses it answered with generic information about the State of Hawaii and never once named the theater. This is not wrong facts about you. It is a failure to see that you exist, and it is the same root cause as the 0% citation in section 1.
  2. Gemma sends people to the wrong website. In fourteen of eighteen responses it gave your address as hawaiitheatre.org. Your official site is hawaiitheatre.com, and the .org did not resolve when we checked it, so anyone who follows Gemma’s answer lands nowhere. This points to an outdated or incorrect link about you somewhere in the public record that the model learned from.

One smaller note. ChatGPT search attached mismatched source links to otherwise-correct event names, for example a mainland comedian’s page for the Steven Ho show. The event facts were right, the citations were careless. Low priority.

5. The punch list

Prioritized by impact, for your team to execute.

  1. Close the Microsoft Copilot blind spot. Copilot and ChatGPT search, your two weakest engines, both rely on the Bing index, and Copilot does not recognize you at all. The highest-leverage move is making hawaiitheatre.com fully visible there: verify the site in Bing Webmaster Tools and confirm the event and venue pages carry complete structured data (PerformingArtsTheater and Event schema).
  2. Correct the wrong-domain signal Gemma learned. Gemma directs people to a dead hawaiitheatre.org instead of your real .com. Track down where that .org association lives in the public record (an old directory listing, a Wikidata or Wikipedia link, a stale press citation) and get it corrected to hawaiitheatre.com. That stray link is what is teaching a model to send people to a dead address.
  3. Protect the editorial advantage that is already working. Claude and Perplexity cite you because of your real history and current coverage. Keep that surface accurate and fresh (press, the Wikipedia entry, event listings) so the training-data lead compounds rather than decays.
  4. Watch the generic-name reseller. The honolulu-theatre.com ticket reseller is capturing category demand. Strong, well-structured event pages on your own domain are the defense, and they double as the fix for item 1.
