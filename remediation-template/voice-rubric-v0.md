# Voice Rubric v0

Pre-publish checklist for any piece of content shipping under a Never Ranked engagement. Run every article through this before flipping `draft: false`.

This is the generic v0. Each client gets their own rubric derived from this one after you've done one voice pass on their actual content.

---

## How to use this rubric

1. Read the article out loud, start to finish, in one pass.
2. At the end of each section, ask yourself ONE question: "Did that sound like the client saying it?"
3. If yes, move on. If no, flag the specific sentence. Don't rewrite yet.
4. After the full read, go back and fix flagged sentences.
5. Run through the rubric checks below. Any fail = do not publish.

---

## Hard fails (any one of these = block publish)

### 1. Em dashes

Em dashes (`—`) are banned in Never Ranked content. Every em dash in the draft must become a period, a comma, or a pair of parens, depending on context.

**Search pattern:** `—` (the actual character, not the hyphen)
**Expected count:** 0

**Why:** Em dashes are the single most reliable tell that content was drafted by an LLM. Every frontier model defaults to em dashes for stylistic variation. Removing them is the easiest, highest-leverage voice intervention.

### 2. Semicolons in marketing copy

Semicolons are banned in marketing/blog content. Replace with periods.

**Search pattern:** `;`
**Expected count:** 0 (outside code blocks)

**Why:** Agents don't write with semicolons. Semicolons signal formal/academic prose. They're a voice mismatch for any client whose audience is working professionals.

### 3. AI filler phrases

Any of these phrases is an automatic fail. Search and destroy.

- "hidden gem"
- "rare opportunity"
- "Welcome to..."
- "Nestled in..."
- "In today's fast-paced world"
- "In the ever-evolving landscape"
- "At its core"
- "It's worth noting that"
- "Delve into"
- "Navigate the complexities"
- "Unlock the potential"
- "Dive deep"
- "A must-have"
- "In the realm of"
- "Harness the power of"
- "Leverage cutting-edge"
- "Game-changer" / "game-changing"
- "Revolutionize"
- "Seamless" / "seamlessly"
- "Robust"
- "Comprehensive suite"

**Why:** These are the verbal tics of AI-generated prose. They don't sound human because humans don't say them. Any surviving filler phrase is a signal the draft wasn't properly voice-passed.

### 4. Meta descriptions that use the title's opening phrase

The meta description cannot start with the same phrase as the article title. Google truncates the snippet and readers see the same text twice.

### 5. Emojis in body content

Never Ranked content is emoji-free except on Instagram captions. Any emoji in article body, headings, or meta is a fail.

## Soft fails (fix before publish but not a blocker)

### 6. Passive voice in the opening paragraph

The first paragraph of any article should be in active voice. If the opening is "It has been observed that..." or "It is often assumed that...", rewrite.

### 7. Hedging phrases in the TL;DR

The TL;DR is meant to be declarative. Phrases like "might", "could potentially", "arguably", "in some cases" belong in the body paragraphs, not in the summary lead.

### 8. Generic CTAs

"Click here", "Learn more", "Contact us today" — all generic. The CTA should describe what actually happens when the reader clicks. Example: "Paste a listing into the free Montaic grader and see what would get flagged" beats "Try Montaic today".

### 9. Unverified numbers

Any stat or figure in the article must have a source. If the draft says "$5,000 median settlement" and there's no citation, either find the source or delete the number. No stat is better than a wrong stat.

### 10. Closing paragraphs that restate the intro

The closing should advance the reader toward action, not rehash what they just read. If the last paragraph sounds like the TL;DR with different words, rewrite it to point the reader at the next step.

## The single question that catches 80% of voice failures

After reading the whole article, ask yourself:

**"If someone handed this to me and said a human wrote it, would I believe them?"**

If you hesitate on that question, the article isn't ready. The hesitation itself is the signal — specific sentences will be off, but your instinct catches it before you can articulate why. Trust the instinct. Go back and read again, flagging what feels off.

## The Hello Momentum tests (apply to marketing content)

For any client whose content routes through the Hello Momentum tastemaking pipeline, these additional tests apply:

### The swap test

Could you swap the client's name out of this content and drop in a competitor? If yes, the content isn't distinctive enough. It's interchangeable marketing sludge.

### The so-what test

After the headline, does the first sentence answer "so what"? If a reader needs three paragraphs to understand why this matters, the lede is wrong.

### The lever test

What creative lever does this piece use? (Examples: contrarian framing, legal authority, insider knowledge, product mechanism reveal, personal origin story.) If you can't name the lever, the content has no mechanism and will read as generic.

### The remove-a-word test

Go through the opening paragraph and try removing one word at a time. If any word can be removed without changing the meaning, remove it. Marketing copy earns its word count; every word must carry load.

## Client-specific additions (how this rubric evolves)

The first voice pass on each client's content will surface client-specific rules. Add them here as you discover them.

**Example — Montaic (client #1):**

- Pronunciation callout: "Montaic (mon-TAY-ik)" should appear at least once per article on first reference
- First-person voice is allowed and encouraged when the author is Lance
- The phrase "The Montaic Method" is never written as "Montaic method" — the capital M in Method is non-negotiable
- Terracotta #C17343 is the product accent color — any color reference in body text must match
- First-reference of "Fair Housing Act" must include "42 U.S.C. § 3604(c)" somewhere in the same section

Each new client adds their own block like this as their voice is learned. After client #3, the rubric gets promoted to v1 with cross-client patterns extracted.
